KOALA

The Koala is a tree-dwelling marsupial native to Australia, most commonly found in the eucalyptus forests of Queensland, New South Wales, Victoria, and South Australia. Despite often being called a "koala bear," it is not a bear at all, but a marsupial, meaning it carries and nurses its young in a pouch.

Koalas have a stout, tailless body with thick, grey fur, a large nose, and fluffy ears. They typically weigh between 4 to 15 kilograms, with males being larger than females. Koalas have strong limbs and sharp claws to help them climb trees and grip branches securely.

Koalas are herbivores and feed almost exclusively on eucalyptus leaves. They are very selective, eating only certain species of eucalyptus, and even then, they pick the youngest, most nutrient-rich leaves. Eucalyptus leaves are low in nutrition and high in toxins, so koalas have a slow metabolism and sleep for up to 18–22 hours a day to conserve energy.

Koalas are mostly solitary animals, each occupying a home range that may overlap with others. They communicate using a range of vocalizations, especially during mating season. Males produce a deep bellowing call to attract females and warn other males.

Breeding typically occurs between September and March. After a gestation period of about 35 days, a single joey is born and crawls into the mother’s pouch. The joey remains in the pouch for about six months, then rides on the mother's back for several more months while continuing to nurse and learn to eat leaves.

Koalas are mostly active at night (nocturnal) and are expert climbers. They are not territorial but do maintain home ranges with favored trees. Koalas spend most of their lives in the trees and rarely come down to the ground.

Threats to koalas include habitat destruction due to urban development, bushfires, climate change, disease (especially chlamydia), and dog attacks. Their populations have declined significantly in recent years, and in some parts of Australia, they are listed as endangered.

Interesting facts about koalas include their highly specialized digestive system, which can detoxify poisonous eucalyptus compounds, and their fingerprints, which are remarkably similar to human fingerprints. Koalas also have a strong sense of smell, which they use to identify suitable eucalyptus leaves.
