GORILLA

Gorillas are the largest primates on Earth and are closely related to humans, sharing about 98% of our DNA. They belong to the genus Gorilla and are native to the forests of central Sub-Saharan Africa. Gorillas are known for their incredible strength, intelligence, and social behavior.

There are two species of gorillas—**Eastern Gorilla** (Gorilla beringei) and **Western Gorilla** (Gorilla gorilla)—each with two subspecies. They inhabit dense forests, swamps, and lowland jungles, depending on the region and subspecies.

Adult male gorillas, known as silverbacks due to the distinctive silver hair on their backs, can weigh between 135 to 220 kilograms and stand around 1.5 to 1.8 meters tall when upright. Females are smaller, typically weighing 70 to 110 kilograms. Gorillas move primarily by knuckle-walking, using their long arms to support their weight.

Gorillas are herbivores and spend most of their day feeding on leaves, stems, fruit, and bamboo shoots. Occasionally, they may eat insects. Their diet varies based on habitat and availability of food, and they rarely drink water directly since they get most of their hydration from the plants they eat.

Gorillas live in family groups called troops, typically led by a dominant silverback male. A troop can include several females, their offspring, and sometimes younger males. The silverback is responsible for leading, making decisions, protecting the group, and resolving conflicts.

Reproduction in gorillas involves a gestation period of about 8.5 months. A single infant is usually born and is cared for intensively by its mother. Infants cling to their mothers for the first few months and begin walking at around 6 to 9 months. Young gorillas are playful and learn social skills through interaction with siblings and other juveniles.

Gorillas are generally gentle and shy but can display aggression when threatened. Silverbacks may beat their chests, roar, or charge to defend their group. Despite their intimidating size, gorillas rarely attack unless provoked.

Sadly, gorillas are critically endangered due to habitat destruction, poaching, disease (like the Ebola virus), and conflict with humans. Conservation efforts are ongoing, including protected reserves, ecotourism, and international laws against wildlife trafficking.

Interesting facts about gorillas include their use of tools in the wild, such as using sticks to measure water depth or gather food. They are highly intelligent and capable of showing emotions like joy, sadness, and empathy. Some gorillas in captivity have even learned elements of sign language to communicate with humans.
