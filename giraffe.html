GIRAFFE (GIRAFFA CAMELOPARDALIS)

The Giraffe is the tallest land animal on Earth and one of the most iconic animals of Africa. Scientifically known as Giraffa camelopardalis, giraffes are known for their extremely long necks, long legs, and distinctive spotted coats. They are native to various parts of sub-Saharan Africa, typically inhabiting savannas, grasslands, and open woodlands.

Adult male giraffes (called bulls) can reach heights of up to 5.5 to 6 meters (18 to 20 feet) and weigh between 1,200 to 1,900 kilograms. Females (cows) are generally slightly shorter and lighter. Their necks alone can be over 2 meters long, containing the same number of vertebrae (seven) as most mammals, though much longer in size.

Giraffes are herbivores, feeding mainly on the leaves, flowers, and fruits of tall trees—especially acacias. Their long tongues (up to 45 cm) and prehensile lips help them grasp and strip leaves from branches. Giraffes often spend most of the day browsing for food and can consume up to 34 kilograms of foliage daily.

Despite their size, giraffes are quite agile and can run at speeds up to 60 km/h over short distances. They are social animals, often found in loose herds, although they do not form strong social bonds like some other animals. Giraffes communicate through various vocalizations, body language, and even infrasound below the human hearing range.

Reproduction in giraffes involves a gestation period of about 15 months, after which a single calf is born. Newborns are already around 1.8 meters tall at birth and can stand and walk within hours. Calves are vulnerable to predators such as lions, hyenas, and leopards, and many do not survive their first year.

Adult giraffes have few natural predators due to their size and powerful kicks, which can be lethal. However, they still need to be cautious, especially when drinking water, as they must awkwardly spread their legs and lower their necks, making them more vulnerable.

Giraffes play a crucial role in their ecosystems by helping control tree growth and spreading seeds through their droppings. Their population is currently declining in some areas due to habitat loss, poaching, and human-wildlife conflict, and they are listed as vulnerable by the IUCN.

Interesting facts about giraffes include their unique coat patterns—no two individuals have the same pattern—and their ability to sleep for only short periods, often standing up. They also have high blood pressure due to their long necks and specialized cardiovascular systems to manage blood flow to their brains.
